All About Reflexes — A Simple Guide for Parents

Primitive reflexes are the automatic movements babies are born with to help them survive and learn (think: rooting to find milk, startle to call for help). As children grow and move—tummy time, rolling, crawling—these early patterns should fade, making room for steadier balance, better focus, and clear speech. When a reflex lingers, it can look like wiggly sitting, picky eating, messy handwriting, or big feelings. This page explains what that means and what gentle steps you can try at home.

Kind reminder: the ideas here support learning and everyday participation. They are not medical advice. Please consult your pediatrician or therapist for personal care.

What are primitive reflexes?

They are quick, automatic movements run by the brainstem that help newborns survive and begin to develop. As children gain head control and start moving on purpose, these reflexes should quiet down so more mature, voluntary skills can take over.

Big idea: reflexes come first to keep babies safe; then they step back so balance, coordination, speech, and attention can grow.

How do reflexes integrate?

Through ordinary movement. Tummy time, rolling, crawling, pulling to stand—each milestone helps the nervous system “file away” a reflex. Some reflexes settle in the first year; a few resolve closer to age three. You can often see a child’s reflexes easing as new milestones appear.

If a reflex stays active, what might you notice?

Every reflex has its own fingerprint. When one hangs on, a child may build awkward work-arounds instead of smooth, automatic skills. That can show up as clumsiness in games, trouble sitting still to learn, emotional over-reactions, or difficulties with reading and handwriting.

Why might a reflex be retained?

There isn’t one proven cause. Factors sometimes noted include prematurity, limited floor time in infancy, high stress or illness, tongue/lip ties, or difficult births. Retention is not anyone’s fault; it’s simply a clue about nervous-system maturity.

Integration timeline (typical range)

Rooting & Suck: usually quiet by ~3–6 months.
Palmar (hand grasp): eases around 3–6 months.
Spinal Galant: typically settles by ~3–9 months.
TLR (head forward/back tone): flexion ~4 months; extension begins ~3–6 months, with maturing thereafter.
Moro (startle): integrates by ~4–6 months.
ATNR (fencer’s pose): settles by ~6 months.
STNR: integrates closer to ~9–11 months.
Landau: may remain into toddler years; generally quiet by ~3 years.

Every child is unique; ranges are guides, not tests. Track overall comfort, participation, and progress over time.

Reflex Library (parent-friendly summaries)

Each card explains what the reflex does, everyday signs it may still be active, and gentle home ideas. Pick 1–2 ideas and practice most days; tiny, consistent steps matter most.

M

Moro Reflex • “Startle”

Purpose: alerts a newborn when something feels sudden.

Possible signs if active: big reactions to small surprises, sensitivity to light/sound/touch, motion sickness, difficulty calming after upsets, impulse control challenges.

Try at home: slow “starfish” opens & curls, deep pressure hugs, rhythmic rocking, calm breathing with long exhales.

A

ATNR • “Fencer’s Pose”

Purpose: early hand-eye linking when the head turns.

Possible signs if active: trouble crossing midline, messy handwriting, letter reversals, copying from board is tiring, mixed hand dominance, ball skills feel hard.

Try at home: cross-crawl marching, lazy-8 tracing, midline games (pass a toy across the body), slow rolling on the floor.

S

STNR

Purpose: helps shift from tummy to hands-and-knees.

Possible signs if active: slumped sitting, W-sit preference, difficulty staying still at a desk, clumsy transitions, swimming and upper-/lower-body coordination feel hard.

Try at home: cat–cow on hands and knees, slow crawl races, wall push-ups, short scooter-board pushes.

G

Spinal Galant

Purpose: helps wriggle through the birth canal and awaken trunk movement.

Possible signs if active: “ants-in-the-pants” fidgeting, bedwetting past age five, distractible sitting, sensitivity to waistbands/tags, difficulty holding attention.

Try at home: snow-angel movements on the floor, playful back brushing (light to firm), swimming kicks on tummy.

T

TLR • Tonic Labyrinthine

Purpose: links head position with body tone.

Possible signs if active: poor balance or spatial judgment, fear of heights, low core strength, fatigue with arms overhead, sequencing and tracking challenges.

Try at home: gentle rocking and rolling games, log rolls, balance-beam lines made with tape, prone “superman” holds.

P

Palmar / Plantar • Grasp

Purpose: automatic grasping for early bonding and stability.

Possible signs if active: pencil grip too tight, hand fatigue, messy eating, overflow mouth movements when writing, toe-walking.

Try at home: squeeze–release putty games, finger “pianos,” barefoot play on varied textures.

R

Rooting & Suck

Purpose: early feeding and calming.

Possible signs if active: lingering drool, picky textures, speech clarity challenges, thumb-sucking beyond toddler years, trouble chewing/swallowing efficiently.

Try at home: blowing through straws/poppers, chewy tubes or crunchy snacks as advised, lip/cheek stretches, sipping thick smoothies with small rests.

How much and how often? Pick 1–2 ideas, 5–10 minutes a day, most days. Keep it playful. Track comfort and function (calmer meals, neater writing, better balance) rather than chasing perfection.

Parent FAQ

Is this a replacement for therapy?

No. Think of it as a daily home routine that complements professional care. If you have concerns about safety, swallowing, pain, or regression, speak to your clinician.

How long until we notice change?

Many families notice small wins in 2–4 weeks when consistent. Bigger changes build over months. Every child’s timeline is unique.

What should we track?

Short notes work great: energy after school, meltdowns, handwriting endurance, balance on stairs, sleep quality, and confidence in new places.
